Strait of Hormuz Crisis: IEA Calls It the Largest Oil Supply Disruption in Market History

The International Energy Agency has characterized the Hormuz closure — now in its 81st day — as "the largest oil supply disruption in the history of global markets," surpassing the 1970s oil shocks. Before the conflict, roughly 25% of the world's seaborne oil trade transited the strait monthly; April traffic collapsed to just 191 vessels, approximately 5% of pre-war levels. Global oil inventories are approaching all-time lows of 7.6 billion barrels by end-May according to UBS, and Iran has formally established the "Persian Gulf Strait Authority" to regulate passage for a fee.

Market Impact

The inventory depletion trajectory is the single most important commodity driver in the medium term; price spikes in Q3 are structurally embedded unless a diplomatic resolution materializes. Refinery crack spreads in Europe (already tightening) risk blowing out as Middle Eastern crude alternatives become scarce. LNG spot prices in Asia will follow. Fertilizer supply chains remain severed (bullish for MOS, NTR). Defense contractors (LMT, RTX, NOC) are beneficiaries of a protracted conflict posture. US domestic shale producers (PXD, COP, EOG) are key swing supply plays.
